## Unit I: Concepts and Perspectives (HSTI)

### 1. Meaning of History
History is the systematic study of the human past based on evidence.  
It explains events, processes, ideas, and institutions over time, not just a list of dates.

### 2. Objectivity in History
Objectivity means presenting the past as accurately as possible by using reliable sources and logical method.  
Complete objectivity is difficult, but historians aim for fairness, verification, and balance.

### 3. Determinism
Determinism is the view that historical events are shaped by prior causes (economic, social, political, cultural).  
It suggests events do not happen randomly, but through identifiable conditions.

Examples:
- Economic determinism (Marx): economic factors drive history
- Geographical determinism: geography shapes societies and events
Criticism: Overemphasis on one factor can oversimplify complex historical realities.

### 4. Relativism
Relativism means understanding the past in its own context.  
Values, beliefs, and actions should be judged in relation to the time and society in which they occurred.

Examples:
- Slavery: accepted in some societies but condemned in others

### 5. Causation in History
Causation studies why events happen.  
Historical events usually have multiple causes:
- Long-term causes (structural background)
- Immediate causes (triggers)
- Contributory causes (supporting factors)

Examples:
- World War I: long-term causes (nationalism, imperialism), immediate
causes (assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand), contributory causes (military alliances,
arms race)

### 6. Generalization in History
Generalization means drawing broader patterns from specific events.  
It helps compare periods and societies, but over-generalization can ignore complexity.

Examples:
- "The Renaissance was a period of great artistic achievement" (generalization)
- "The Renaissance was a complex era with both artistic innovation and social inequality" (more nuanced generalization)

### 7. Moral Judgment in History
Moral judgment is evaluating past actions in ethical terms.  
Historians must balance ethical reflection with contextual understanding and evidence-based analysis.

Examples:
- Evaluating colonialism: recognizing exploitation while understanding historical context
- Evaluating leaders: acknowledging achievements while critiquing moral failings (e.g., Gandhi's views on race)

### 8. Extent of Subjectivity
Subjectivity enters through:
- Choice of topic
- Selection of sources
- Language and interpretation

Good history reduces subjectivity through source criticism, cross-checking, and transparent reasoning.

Example: Two historians writing about the same event (e.g., French Revolution) may have different interpretations based on their perspectives, but both can be valid if supported by evidence.

### 9. History vs Physical Sciences
History:
- Deals with human actions and unique contexts
- Relies on interpretation of incomplete evidence
- Cannot run controlled experiments

Physical sciences:
- Focus on repeatable experiments
- Seek universal laws with measurable outcomes

### 10. Interpretation and Speculation
- Interpretation: Reasoned explanation from evidence
- Speculation: Unsupported assumption beyond evidence

Historical writing should prioritize interpretation and minimize speculation.

Example:
- Interpretation: "The economic crisis of 1929 contributed to the rise of extremist politics in Germany."
- Speculation: "The economic crisis of 1929 was the sole reason for the rise of extremist politics in Germany."

### 11. Causation vs Evidence
Causation claims must be grounded in evidence.  
Without documentary/material support, causal explanation becomes weak or speculative.

Examples:
- Causation with evidence: "The Treaty of Versailles imposed harsh reparations on Germany, which contributed to economic instability and political unrest."
- Causation without evidence: "The Treaty of Versailles was the only reason for Germany's problems in the 1930s." (ignoring other factors like global depression, internal politics,etc.)

### 12. Historical Inevitability
Historical inevitability is the idea that some events were bound to occur.  
Modern historiography treats this cautiously because history also contains contingency, choices, and alternatives.

Examples:
- The fall of the Roman Empire: often seen as inevitable due to internal decay and external pressures, but alternative outcomes were possible with different leadership or policies.
- The rise of democracy: often viewed as inevitable in the modern world, but it could have taken different forms or been delayed under different circumstances.

### 14. Science and Technology: Meaning, Scope, Importance
- Science: Systematic knowledge of natural/social phenomena through observation, logic, and testing.
- Technology: Practical application of knowledge, skills, and tools to solve problems.

Scope:
- Agriculture, medicine, transport, communication, industry, defense, environment, and digital systems.

Importance:
- Improves quality of life
- Raises productivity
- Supports social and economic development
- Strengthens national capability

### 15. Interaction of Science, Technology, and Society
Science, technology, and society influence each other:
- Society creates needs and priorities
- Science creates new knowledge
- Technology applies that knowledge
- New technologies reshape economy, culture, ethics, and policy

### 16. Sources for History of Science and Technology in India
Major sources include:
- Archaeological remains and artifacts
- Inscriptions and coins
- Manuscripts and classical texts
- Technical treatises (medicine, mathematics, astronomy, metallurgy, etc.)
- Travel accounts and chronicles
- Colonial administrative/technical records
- Institutional archives, journals, patents, reports
- Oral traditions and local knowledge systems
